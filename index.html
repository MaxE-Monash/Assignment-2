Health across Australia and the Globe

Health Expenditure



Globally, we can see over the span of 9 years from 2010-2019, we can see that generally expenditure on health increased across nearly all nations . Africa’s increase in spending has been proportionally less, with some nations spending regressing or stagnating over the ten year period. A notable example of this is in "Chad".

Europe seemed to be the most consistently affluent region when it comes to expenditure. The United States of America seemed to expend the most money on health per person, with an expenditure of around ~11 thousand dollars per person in 2019.

Key Facts

• Over the 9 Year period Australia's spending increase by around ~47%

• Largest single year increase was in 2018, with a jump of ~400 dollars per individual in Australia

• Australia and New Zealand tended to dominate the Oceania's region expenditure, with an expenditure more than double its any other oceanic inhabitant

• Life Expectancy tended to be significantly larger in countries where Expenditure was high

COVID-19 Daily Averages in Australia

Over the course of the COVID-19 pandemic, Australia’s daily rate of Covid-19 hospitalizations per 1 million people were recorded. This recording began on March 31st of 2020. The trends of hospitalizations seen in 2020, were strongly marked by the general trends to utilize lockdowns to prevent the further spread of the virus. This alongside fears of overwhelming the hospital capacity, drove infections down, reducing the number of hospitalizations down. .

• In 2023 ,January was the month with the largest number of hospitalizations, approximately 107 hospitalizations per million per day.

• In 2022, was marked by severely more hospitalizations per million compared to previous years. With an average number of hospitalizations of 115 in January ,117 in July and 145 in August

• 2020 had relatively small amounts of hospitalizations, with the number of cases in 2020 spiking to around ~23 hospitalizations per million per day in August .

• In 2021, The number of hospitalizations peaked on December 31, with 61 hospitalizations per million people in Australia.

Infant Mortality Rate(%) Around the Globe


This visualization displays Health Expenditure per capita versus Infant Mortality rate in 2021 compared against population size and continent. Generally, The trends that we find is that higher rates of GDP expenditure strongly correlate with reduced infant mortality rate.

While there are a few notable exceptions with high amounts of expenditure and relatively high levels of infant mortality such as Palua and Nauru we see that the trend generally holds.
Australia (0.39%) is amongst the nations with the lowest rates of infant mortality , having lower rates compared to both Switzerland (0.49%) and The United States of America (0.68%)

Key Facts
• African Countries tended to have the highest rates of infant mortality, while also having the lowest amount of GDP per capita health expenditure .

• Australian and New Zealand seemed to be outliers when compared to other countries within Oceania, being the only two nations to have below 1 % infant mortality rate.

• Asia had the most broad distribution of nations in both low and high mortality rates.

• European countries tended to have significantly lower infant mortaliy rates while also having higher expenditure

Hospital Locations across Australia

The Visualization above displays the distribution of both Private and Public Hospitals across the entirety of Australia alongside displaying the number of Hospital Admissions per 1000 people . Typically, what we find broadly is that Hospitals are most densely found in the most populous regions of the country. Sparser areas like the Northern Territory or Tasmania have significantly fewer hospitals compared to Victoria or New South Wales

Private ospitals are found closer towards larger metropolitan cities . As there is likely to be a greater profit incentive to do so, alongside with an easier time finding staff. This would explain why there is a large density of Private hospitals within these dense regions.
Notably, Data for the Private sector was not present for Northern Territory , the ACT and Tasmania .

Interestingly, The Northern Territory was found to have the highest amounts of admissions per 1000 thousand people . With a value of 740.36 admissions per thousand people , within in the Public sector. This was significantly more than the second largest amount in the Public sector, being Queensland with approximately 333.66 admissions per thousand people.


The difference may be caused by the lack of Private hospitals in the Northern Territory , causing more elective surgeries to also be covered by the Public hospitals, inflating admissions.

The Hospitals listed amongst the map are commonly listed as amongst the best in the entirety of Australia.

State Expenditure per capita by State and Population

The visualization on the left displays the overall trends of Health Expenditure per capita by Year, State and Population The overlying trends of the data has Southern Australia displaying the highest rates of Expenditure per capita until 2018. Following 2018, the Australian Capital Territories rose by about 6 hundred dollars each year until 2020.

Southern Australia's expenditure in 2016 was remarkably larger than all other states across all following and previous years. This was due to a one-off development project occurring, causing expenditure per person to surge by ~1.6k.

Northern Territory tended to have the least amount of expenditure, both overall and per person. 2011 was the only year where expenditure was following the trends of other states, with xpenditure being 7119.54 per person. Following that year, there was a significant reduction in expenditure until 2016-2017, where it once again began to fall.

Generally, all other states expenditure seemed to form a line from ~7200-500$ per person in 2010, to ~8500$ in 2020. This seems to suggest that perhaps smaller territories could be likely to be more variable in their expenditure.
